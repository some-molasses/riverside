On May 17, 1884, Jumbo descended from the Brooklyn Bridge, into Brooklyn crowds of a million dreams.

[blockquote](The sea of human faces that greeted us as we left the bridge to promenade the Brooklyn streets and avenues was a sight never to be forgotten.

I know of nothing in the world equal to the sight which I took in at the moment I looked at the uplifted faces of that mighty crowd of the human family.)(Matthew Scott, The Autobiography of Jumbo, 1885)

As Jumbo and Scott emerged from the downtown, bodies overflowed from every pore of Fulton Street. People leaned out from the balconies, they poured from the roof-inset garret windows, they gathered from the rooftops above, each one dreaming of a view. 

One dreamer stood out among them. On Fulton Street, a young girl found a crowded fourth-floor window, and began to wait. Blackbirds cawed in the morning sky. Pigeons collect on the steeples beyond. At the end of Fulton Street, a great shadow emerged.

The girl tried to see from her fourth storey window, but couldn’t. She reached further, she stretched, but to little avail. Jumbo came closer. She needed to see. Too many people, too crowded, if only she could get past them. Jumbo came even closer. She sat on the windowsill. She leaned out. A little more. Just to see. A little more—

She overreaches, falls four storeys, and dies on impact.

![image](/works/writing/mathnews/jumbo/jumbo-pet.jpg)

Jumbo walked on. Matthew Scott, alongside, only learned long after of the accident. He describes being haunted by the thought of her mother, racing to the bustling street below, weeping as she “raised up the shattered remains of her darling child.”

On that abrupt note, his autobiography ends.

❦

With Alice, Jumbo’s elephant wife, abandoned across the sea, Scott was Jumbo’s only remaining friend. 

Scott, earlier in his autobiography, describes the litany of injuries he suffered by Jumbo during their travels. Jumbo’s restraints were often flung against Scott, leaving him with severe gashes. He bore physical traumas after being unexpectedly squeezed between the metres-high elephant and a traincar wall. His toe was smashed after the full weight of an elephant fell upon it. 

No injury impacted their bond. It was unbreakable. When danger came met them in Iowa in 1883, Jumbo ensured Scott's safety:

[blockquote](A noise, like the bursting of a thunder-storm, caused me to look around in alarm. It was made by thirty elephants stampeding. They had broken their chains and were smashing everything in their way. In a moment they rushed into our tent. If death ever stared me in the face, it did at that moment.

On came the black mass of mad animals, and I thought there was no escape from being crushed beneath their heavy feet, when Jumbo came to the rescue. He twined his trunk about my body like a flash, and placed me out of harm's way between his legs; then stood firmly and stretched out his trunk, as rigid as the limb of a large tree, and permitted not an elephant to get past it.)(Matthew Scott, The Autobiography of Jumbo, 1885)

So they travelled North America: the Englishman and his pachyderm son, king of the elephants.

❦

While railroads continued to connect the disparate cities of the Great Lakes, one junction city stood as the intersection: a small town in Ontario, halfway between London and Lake Erie.

The Railway City of St. Thomas.

On September 17, 1885, the Barnum and Bailey circus traveled from Chatham to St. Thomas, performing for the St. Thomas crowds that night. By 9:30 PM, the show was over. All but two elephants had been loaded into their railway cars, ready to depart. Matthew Scott walked the remaining two along the tracks to their car: a dwarf elephant named Tom Thumb, and the king of elephants, Jumbo. As they walked atop the wooden railroad ties, Scott looked behind his group, and on the rails, saw a train.

Moving.

Fast.

Its headlight was less than half a kilometer away. The engineer, equally startled, whistled for his crew to engage the brakes, he reversed the engine with utmost speed, but the train was heavy, and going downhill.

Scott told the elephants to run. The three ran down the tracks, encased left and right by fences, hoping to simply outrun the train. 

The train struck Tom Thumb first. Little Tom Thumb was flung from the rails.

The train struck Jumbo next.

St. Thomas heard an elephant roar that night as the train struck Jumbo's hind legs then careened atop his back. As the train derailed, it dragged him, bloodied, several hundred metres before at last coming to a stop.

Scott raced up to his elephant, but Jumbo was ruined. His side bore deep gashes, his feet were torn, blood flowed from his open mouth. He groaned, laying on his side atop the railway tracks. For fifteen minutes he lie bleeding, Scott at his side, helpless to save him.

Jumbo, king of the elephants, had met his fate.

[blockquote](He was given to me a baby, and I have been more than a father to him, for I have performed the duties and bestowed the affections of a mother as far as my humble ability would permit. I am proud of my boy, “Jumbo.” All the experience shared with him is a pleasure to me, and is a great reward, which I am thankful for in these my older days. I feel them creeping on, but so long as I am permitted the company of dear old Jumbo, I shall be quite contented.

I know this, that I am happy in his company, and do not wish to leave it.)(Matthew Scott, The Autobiography of Jumbo, 1885)

News reports say that in his last moments, Jumbo reached out to his keeper. He died with his trunk grasping the hand of the man who called Jumbo his son.

Long thereafter, Matthew Scott laid atop Jumbo’s body and wept.
